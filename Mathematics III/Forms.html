Forms of Differential Equations

Ordinary Differential Equations (ODE)

Come in the form of: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x)$$ To solve these: \begin{align*} dy &= f(x) \ dx & &\text{Multply by $dx$} \\ \int dy &= \int f(x) \ dx + C & &\text{Integrate both sides}\\ y &= \int f(x) \ dx + C \end{align*} Apply initial conditions (ie find $C$) if given.

The acceleration of a particle is defined as $a(t)=t^2-t+1$, its initial position is $x(0)=2$, and its initial velocity is $v(0)=1$. Find the position function $x(t)$
As we know: \begin{align*} &\text{position} & &=x(t) \\ &\text{velocity} & &=\frac{dx}{dt} \\ &\text{acceleration} & &=\frac{d^2t}{dt^2} \text{or} \frac{dv}{dt} \end{align*} We have to find the velocity function first. \begin{align*} a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} &= t^2-t+1 \\ dv &= [t^2-t+1] \ dt \\ \int dv &= \int[t^2-t+1] \ dt \\ v(t) &= \frac{1}{3}t^3-\frac{1}{2}t^2+t+C_1 \\ \end{align*} We have to solve for the initial condition of $C_1$ given $v(0)=1$. \begin{align*} v(0) &= 1 \\ 1 &= \frac{1}{3}(0)^3-\frac{1}{2}(0)^2+(0)+C_1 \\ C_1 &= 1 \end{align*} Our velocity function is $v(t)=\frac{1}{3}t^3-\frac{1}{2}t^2+t+1$. We can find the position function in a similar manner. \begin{align*} v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} &= \frac{1}{3}t^3-\frac{1}{2}t^2+t+1 \\ dx &= [\frac{1}{3}t^3-\frac{1}{2}t^2+t+1] \ dt \\ \int dx &= \int[\frac{1}{3}t^3-\frac{1}{2}t^2+t+1] \ dt \\ x(t) &= \frac{1}{12}t^4-\frac{1}{6}t^3+\frac{1}{2}t^2+t+C_2 \\ \end{align*} We have to again solve for the initial condition of $C_2$ given $x(0)=2$. \begin{align*} x(0) &= 2 \\ 2 &= \frac{1}{12}(0)^4-\frac{1}{6}(0)^3+\frac{1}{2}(0)^2+(0)+C_2 \\ C_1 &= 2 \end{align*} $$ \therefore x(t)=\frac{1}{12}t^4-\frac{1}{6}t^3+\frac{1}{2}t^2+t+2 $$

Variable Seperable Differential Equations (VSDE)

Come in the form of: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x)g(y)$$ To solve these: \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{g(y)} &= f(x) \ dx & &\text{Multiply by $dx$ and divide by $g(y)$} \\ \int \frac{dy}{g(y)} &= \int f(x) \ dx + C & &\text{Integrate both sides} \end{align*} Apply initial conditions (ie find $C$) if given.

Solve the IVP given $\displaystyle y'-\frac{3-2x}{y}=0$ and $y(2)=-4$.
\begin{align*} y'-\frac{3-2x}{y} &= 0 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{3-2x}{y} \\ y \ dy &= [3-2x] \ dx \\ \int y \ dy &= \int [3-2x] \ dx \\ \frac{1}{2}y^2 &= 3x-x^2+C \\ y^2 &= 6x-2x^2+2C \\ y &= \pm \sqrt{6x-2x^2+2C} \end{align*} Now we have to solve for the initial value (ie $C$). Notice how the positive in the plus or minus is inadmissable to due the fact that it cannot equal $-4$ when positive. \begin{align*} y(2) &= -4 \\ -4 &= -\sqrt{6(2)-2(2)^2+2C} \\ 4 &= \sqrt{12-8+2C} \\ 16 &= 4+2C \\ 12 &= 2C \\ \end{align*} Notice how we don't need to solve for what $C$ is, only $2C$, as it is still a constant. $$ \therefore y=-\sqrt{6x-2x^2+12} $$

Linear Differential Equations (LDE)

Come in the form of: $$\frac{dy}{dx}+P(x)y=Q(x)$$ To solve these: $$\text{Multiply by $\displaystyle e^{\int P(x) \ dx}$}$$ The left hand side will become a sum of two factors similar to the product rule when finding derivatives.
Apply initial conditions (ie find $C$) if given.

Solve $\displaystyle x^2y'+2xy=\sin^2x$.
We have to get $y'$ but itself for it to be in its proper form. \begin{align*} x^2y'+2xy &= \sin^2x & I(x) &= e^{\int \frac{2}{x} \ dx} \\ y'+\frac{2y}{x} &= \frac{\sin^2x}{x^2} & &= e^{2\ln|x|} \\ x^2y'+2xy &= \sin^2(x) & &= x^2 \\ \frac{d(x^2y)}{dx} &= \sin^2x & &\text{Take note that it will always take the form of $\displaystyle \frac{df}{dx}$} \\ \int d(x^2y) &= \int\sin^2x \ dx \\ x^2y &= \int\frac{1}{2}(1-\cos (2x)) \ dx \\ x^2y &= \int(\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{2}\cos (2x)) \ dx \\ x^2y &= \frac{1}{2}x-\frac{\sin(2x)}{4} + C\\ y &= \frac{1}{2x}-\frac{\sin(2x)}{4x^2} + \frac{C}{x^2}\\ \end{align*}

Homogeneous Differential Equations (LDE)

Come in the form of: \begin{align*} P(x,y)y' &= Q(x,y) & &\text{where $P$ and $Q$ have the same total degree} \end{align*} To solve these: \begin{align*} &\text{Get into the form of} & \frac{dy}{dx} &= F\frac{y}{x} \\ &\text{Make the substitution}& v &= \frac{y}{x} \\ & & y &= vx \\ & & dy &= v'x + v \end{align*} These will become VSDE and we can continue from there.
Apply initial conditions (ie find $C$) if given.

Solve $\displaystyle xyy'=y^2-3x^2$.
\begin{align*} xyy' &= y^2-3x^2 & &\text{Divide by $x^2$ to be able to substitute} \\ \frac{y}{x}y' &= \bigg(\frac{y}{x}\bigg)-3 & &\text{Substitute by $v=\frac{y}{x}$ and $y'=v'x+v$} \\ v(v'x+v) &= v^2-3 \\ v'x+v &= \frac{v^2-3}{v} \\ \frac{dv}{dx}x &= \frac{v^2-3}{v}-v \\ \frac{x}{dx} &= \frac{v^2-3-v^2}{v} \cdot \frac{1}{dv} \\ \frac{dx}{x} &= \frac{v}{-3} \ dv \\ \int v \ dv &= \int -3 \frac{dx}{x} \\ \frac{1}{2}v^2 &= -3\ln|x|+ln|C| \\ v^2 &= 2\ln\bigg|\frac{C}{x^3}\bigg| \\ v &= \pm\sqrt{2\ln\bigg|\frac{C}{x^3}\bigg|} \\ \frac{y}{x} &= \pm\sqrt{2\ln\bigg|\frac{C}{x^3}\bigg|} \\ y &= \pm x \sqrt{2\ln\bigg|\frac{C}{x^3}\bigg|} \end{align*}

Bernoulli Differential Equations

Come in the form of: \begin{align*} y'+P(x)y &= O(x)y^n & &\text{where $n \neq 0,1$} \end{align*} To solve these: \begin{align*} &\text{Divide by $y^n$} \\ &\text{Substitute by $v=y^{1-n}$} \end{align*} These will become LDE and we can continue from there.
Apply initial conditions (ie find $C$) if given.

Solve $\displaystyle y'+\frac{2y}{x}=\frac{y^3}{x^2}$.
We can see that this is Bernoulli and is $n=3$. \begin{align*} y'+\frac{2y}{x} &= \frac{y^3}{x^2} & v = y^{1-3} &\rightarrow v = \frac{1}{y^2} \\ -\frac{1}{2}v^{-\tfrac{3}{2}}v' + \frac{2 \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{v}}}{x} &= \frac{\Big(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{v}}\Big)^3}{x^2} & y^2 = \frac{1}{v} &\rightarrow y = \frac{1}{\sqrt{v}}\\ v' + \frac{2}{x\sqrt{v}}\cdot\frac{1}{-\tfrac{1}{2}v^{-\tfrac{3}{2}}} &= \frac{1}{x^2v^{\tfrac{3}{2}}} \cdot \frac{1}{-\frac{1}{2}v^{-\tfrac{3}{2}}} & y' &= -\frac{1}{2}v^{-\tfrac{3}{2}} \\ v' + \frac{2}{x\sqrt{v}}\cdot\Big(-2v^{\tfrac{3}{2}}\Big) &= \frac{-2v^{\tfrac{3}{2}}}{x^2v^{\tfrac{3}{2}}} \\ v' - \frac{4v}{x} &= -\frac{2}{x^2} \\ \end{align*} This is a LDE so we can follow those steps. \begin{align*} v' - \frac{4v}{x} &= -\frac{2}{x^2} & I(x) &= e^{\int 4\tfrac{1}{x} \ dx} \\ \frac{v'}{x^4} - \frac{4v}{x^5} &= -\frac{2}{x^6} & &= e^{4\ln|x|} \\ \frac{d\Big(\tfrac{1}{x^4}v\Big)}{dx} &= -\frac{2}{x^6} & &= x^4 \\ \int d\bigg(\frac{v}{x^4}\bigg) &= \int -\frac{2}{x^6} \ dx \\ \frac{v}{x^4} &= -2 \cdot -\frac{x^{-5}}{-5} + C \\ v &= \frac{2}{5x} + Cx^4 \\ \frac{1}{y^2} &= \frac{2}{5x} + Cx^4 \\ y^2 &= \frac{1}{\tfrac{2}{5x} + Cx^4} \\ y &= \frac{1}{\pm\sqrt{\tfrac{2}{5x} + Cx^4}} \\ \end{align*}

Exact Differential Equations (EDE)

Come in the form of: \begin{align*} M(x,y) \ dx + N(x,y) \ dy = 0 \end{align*} \begin{align*} &\text{Are exact if} & M_y &= N_x & \text{i.e.} \ \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} &= \frac{\partial N}{\partial x} \end{align*} To solve these: \begin{align*} &\text{Divide by $y^n$} \\ &\text{Substitute by $v=y^{1-n}$} \end{align*} These will become LDE and we can continue from there.
Apply initial conditions (ie find $C$) if given.
