Areas between Curves

$$A=\int_a^b[f(x)-g(x)] \ dx$$ If there are regions where $f(x) < g(x)$, then it is necessary to find the intercepts and do the integration through the regions separately. $$A=\int_a^b[f(x)-g(x)] \ dx + \int_b^c[g(x)-f(x)] \ dx + \dots$$ $$A=\int_a^b|f(x)-g(x)| \ dx$$ Sometimes the area can be more easily calculated by defining a function in terms of $y$. $$A=\int_c^d|f(y)-g(y)| \ dy$$

Volumes of Revolution

\begin{align*} V&=\int_a^bA(x) \ dx & V&=\int_a^bA(y) \ dy \end{align*}

Find the volume of a sphere.
The volume is defined by $V=\int_a^bA(x) \ dx$. We have to find what $A(x)$ is.
In a circle, the radius of each area is defined by Pythagoras's theorem, $f(x) = \sqrt{r^2-x^2}$. A circle would be defined as $A = \pi r^2 \rightarrow A(x) = \pi [f(x)]^2$ \begin{align*} A(x) &= \pi \bigg[\sqrt{r^2-x^2}\bigg]^2 \\ &= \pi [r^2-x^2] \end{align*} Now we will integrate from $-r$ to $r$ since that is the radius of the sphere is. \begin{align*} V &= \int_{-r}^r \pi [r^2-x^2] \ dx \\ &= 2\pi \int_0^r [r^2-x^2] \ dx \\ &= 2\pi [r^2x - \frac{1}{3}x^3]\bigg|_0^r \\ &= 2\pi [r^2(r) - \frac{1}{3}(r)^3] \\ &= 2\pi \frac{2}{3}r^3 \\ &= \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \end{align*}

Methods of Finding Volume

Disks

If the volume is a solid with no holes, then the the area can be found through: \begin{align*} A(x) &= \pi [f(x)]^2 & &\leftarrow & A &= \pi r^2 & &\rightarrow & A(y) &= \pi [f(y)]^2 \end{align*} Washers
If the volume is a solid with a circular hole, then the the area can be found through: \begin{align*} A(x) &= \pi [f(x)]^2 - \pi [g(x)]^2 & &\leftarrow & A &= \pi r_{out}^2 - \pi r_{in}^2& &\rightarrow & A(y) &= \pi [f(y)]^2 - \pi [g(y)]^2 \end{align*} When not rotating around the x or y axis, $f and g$ will be shifted. The difference between the axis of rotation and the function will be your radius.

Cylindrical Shells
A volume may be more easily found by cutting the solid into small cylinders with a cylindrical hole in the middle (cylindrical shell). The volume of the shell can be defined as: $$V=2\pi r \ h \ \Delta r$$

$2\pi r$
The circumference of the shell
$h$
The height of the shell
$\Delta r$
The thickness of the shell
The sum of all these volumes would result in the actual volume. $$V=\int_a^b 2\pi x \ f(x) \ dx$$
$2\pi x$
The circumference of the shell, where $x$ is the distance from the centre to the shell
$h$
The height of the shell
$dx$
The infinitesimally small thickness of the shell
This is can be similarly said for when rotating around the x-axis, $V=\int_c^d 2\pi x \ g(y) \ dy$

Find the volume defined by $x=y$ and $x=\sqrt[3]{y}$, rotated about the y-axis.

As you can see, the intercepts are at $y = 0,1$. However, we must always confirm it is. \begin{align*} y &= \sqrt[3]{y} \\ y^3 &= y \\ 0 &= y^3 - y \\ 0 &= y^2(y-1) \\ y &= 0,1 \end{align*}
In this situation, it is best to use washers since there is a hole in the middle but cylindrical shells would work just as well. As well, it is best to define the functions in terms of y since the washes will rotate around the y-axis. \begin{align*} A &= \pi r_{out}^2 - \pi r_{in}^2 & V &= \int_0^1 [\pi \sqrt[3]{y}^2 - \pi y^2] \ dy \\ A(y) &= \pi [f(y)]^2 - \pi [g(y)]^2 & &= \pi [\frac{3}{5}y^{\tfrac{5}{3}}-\frac{1}{3}y^3]\bigg|_0^1 & V &= \frac{4\pi}{15}\\ A(y) &= \pi [\sqrt[3]{y}]^2 - \pi [y]^2 & &= \pi [\frac{3}{5}y^{\tfrac{5}{3}}-\frac{1}{3}y^3]\bigg|_0^1 \\ \end{align*}

Find the volume defined by $y=x(x-1)^2$ between $0\leq x\leq 1$.

Using washers would not be a good idea since you'll have to split the volume into two separate solids and add them up together again. An easier method would be to use cylindrical shells which were defined as: $$V=\int_a^b 2\pi x \ f(x) \ dx$$

 

\begin{align*} V &= \int_a^b 2\pi x \ f(x) \ dx \\ &= \int_0^1 2\pi x \ [x(x-1)^2] \ dx \\ &= 2\pi\int_0^1 x[x(x^2-2x+1)] \ dx & V &= \frac{\pi}{15}\\ &= 2\pi\int_0^1 [x^4-2x^3+x^2] \ dx \\ &= 2\pi[\frac{1}{5}x^5-\frac{2}{4}x^4+\frac{1}{3}x^3]\bigg|_0^1 \\ &= 2\pi[\frac{1}{5}1^5-\frac{2}{4}1^4+\frac{1}{3}1^3\bigg] \\ \end{align*}

Arc Lengths

\begin{align*} L &= \int_a^b\sqrt{1+\bigg[\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg]^2 } \ dx & L &= \int_c^d\sqrt{1+\bigg[\frac{dx}{dy}\bigg]^2 } \ dy \end{align*} Arc Length Functions
You can also integrate from a constant to a variable. This would result in an arc length function. $$L(x) = \int_a^x\sqrt{1+[f'(t)]^2 } \ dt$$

Find the length of the curve defined by $\displaystyle y=\frac{1}{4}x^2-\frac{1}{2}\ln{x}$ between $1\leq x\leq 3$.
First we have to find the derivative of the function. $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{2}x-\frac{1}{2x}$$ Determining what $\displaystyle 1+\bigg[\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg]^2$ first is a clearer way of finding what the length is. It is recommended as a sub-procedure but not necessary. \begin{align*} 1+\bigg[\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg]^2 &= 1 + \bigg[\frac{1}{2}x-\frac{1}{2x}\bigg]^2 \\ &= 1 + \frac{1}{4}x^2 - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4x^2} \\ &= \bigg[\frac{1}{2}x+\frac{1}{2x}\bigg]^2 \end{align*} Substitute it back into the original integral. \begin{align*} L &= \int_1^3\sqrt{\bigg[\frac{1}{2}x+\frac{1}{2x}\bigg]^2} \ dx \\ &= \int_1^3\bigg[\frac{1}{2}x+\frac{1}{2x}\bigg]\ dx & &= 2+\frac{1}{2}\ln{3} \\ &= \bigg[\frac{1}{4}x^2+\frac{1}{2}\ln{|x|}\bigg]\bigg|_1^3 \\ &= \frac{1}{4}(3^2) + \frac{1}{2}\ln{|3|} - \frac{1}{4}(1^2) - \frac{1}{2}\ln{|1|} \end{align*}

Find the length of the curve defined by $\displaystyle y=t^2-\frac{1}{8}\ln{t}$ from point, $P_0(1,1)$.
First we have to find the derivative of the function. $$\frac{dy}{dt} = 2t-\frac{1}{8t}$$ Find $\displaystyle 1+\bigg[\frac{dy}{dt}\bigg]^2$. \begin{align*} 1+\bigg[\frac{dy}{dt}\bigg]^2 &= 1 + \bigg[2x-\frac{1}{8t}\bigg]^2 \\ &= 1 + 4x^2 - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{64t^2} \\ &= \bigg[2x+\frac{1}{8t}\bigg]^2 \end{align*} Substitute it back into the original integral. This time, we are integrating from the given point (1,1), to x. \begin{align*} L &= \int_1^x\sqrt{\bigg[2t+\frac{1}{8t}\bigg]^2} \ dt \\ &= \int_1^x\bigg[2t + \frac{1}{8t}\bigg]\ dy & &= x + \frac{1}{8}\ln{|x|} - 1 \\ &= \bigg[t^2 + \frac{1}{8}\ln{|t|}\bigg]\bigg|_1^x \\ &= x^2 + \frac{1}{8}\ln{|x|} - 1^2 - \frac{1}{8}\ln{|1|} \end{align*}

Area of Surface of Revolution

\begin{align*} S &= \int_a^b 2\pi y \ ds \quad \text{about x-axis} & &\text{where} & ds &= \sqrt{1+\bigg[\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg]^2 } \ dx \quad \text{or} \\ S &= \int_c^d 2\pi x \ ds \quad \text{about y-axis} & & & ds &= \sqrt{1+\bigg[\frac{dx}{dy}\bigg]^2 } \ dy \end{align*} Notice how either $ds$ formula could work for the first equations.

Moments \& Centers of Mass

\begin{align*} \bar{x} &= \frac{1}{A} \int_a^b x f(x) \ dx & &\text{Similarily,} & \bar{x} &= \frac{1}{A} \int_c^d y g(y) \ dy \\ \bar{y} &= \frac{1}{A} \int_a^b \frac{1}{2}[f(x)]^2 \ dx & & & \bar{y} &= \frac{1}{A} \int_c^d \frac{1}{2}[g(y)]^2 \ dy \end{align*}

$$\text{where} A=\int_a^b f(x) \text{or} A=\int_c^d g(y)$$
